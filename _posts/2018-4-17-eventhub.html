Introduction

In a city that is oversaturated with weekend events and festivals, Chicagoans need a succinct and efficient way to access a database of information regarding these specific events. Users want to know more than just the date and time of their favorite events and this is where EventHub comes in. The general challenge we are addressing is the wide variety of grievances that attendees have with these kinds of events as well as attempting to create an efficient hub of information regarding these events that will not force users to navigate too far from their current workflow (i.e social media). These grievances include but are not limited to: cost of event / vendor costs, accessibility by public transportation, and overcrowding (or under-crowding). EventHub addresses these grievances by offering site-specific information about a given festival on an easily navigated platform allowing the user to quickly decide whether this is an event they would like to attend or not.

Overview and Features

The design goals that paved the way for most of the decisions we made were primarily based on ease of use and accessibility of specific information. That said, we specifically want to include information regarding breakdown / location of attendees so that users could see approximately how many people were at the event at a given time as well as predict roughly how many people would be there when they chose to arrive. We also wanted to include a way for users to gather information about vendors that would be tabling and their price breakdowns from within the app itself. We wanted these goals to be addressed in tandem with the desire for a location functionality that linked you to festivals that were in your area based on a programmable radius from the user. Our prototype will demonstrate our early iterations of how to best address these goals, which were then further developed in our design process. We wanted to create a streamlined process for the user that had only 1-3 screens between them and their ultimate goal no matter what the goal is. These early examples were clunky and not the most efficient for user experience and so in our secondary prototyping stages we fixed many issues by adding interactive maps earlier into the interface as well as designing multiple ways for a user to link to other pages and get back home so that no matter where they were they could easily navigate to a variety of other pages.

To address many of our design goals, we quickly realized that an interactive map would be the necessary centerpiece of our application. This interactive map is our main feature and contains selectable vendor lists and locations, breakdowns of where nearby public transportation is located (including Divvy station breakdowns of what the ratio of Divvy Bike : Open Spot is) and a live updated map of attendee dispersal throughout the event. The map can be used for all basic information regarding stages of performing artists and entrances and exits, but we felt that an interactive map to this extent would centralize all necessary information into one easy location. Another useful feature is the ability to save events that interest you into a calendar that can send you notifications regarding the festival as it nears (ex. Reminders of when it is, updates to the performing artists schedules, free ticket opportunities, etc.). This offers value to the user because they no longer have to hold information about these events in their heads, but can rather rely on a calendar that will remind them as well as make them aware of opportunities that surround the event.

Reflection

As two people who transferred into this major a bit later than some others, it was scary to try and find internships without having as competitive of portfolios as other peers in our class and grade since they had several quarters of experience over us. However, this designing process allowed us to create something that is portfolio-ready. Although further revision is necessary, we feel comfortable showing employers a prototype that is higher fidelity prototype than what we have built in previous courses. It is apparent that we still have a ways to go, but now employers can track our growth as designers and this assignment shows our capabilities and potential more than any work we have completed thus far.

This assignment was helpful, but could have benefited from a more complex usability testing element. Perhaps we could have done more usability testing with harsher critiques in order to build our application at a more expedited rate. This was obviously due to the constraint of time, but would have nonetheless been beneficial to us as designers. If we could have looked at some more minor, visual elements, this application could have been much stronger and perhaps even more visually engaging. Additionally, we have had such minimal training in Axure and other prototyping software that it was difficult to simply pick up all the knowledge we needed to make our application interactive. Some ideas were just too complex to execute and this hindered our ability to make the application we truly wanted to make. If we could have had more workshops that went in-depth into many softwares, perhaps we would have been able to execute more complex features. However, we realize that this is another constraint of time and resources.

Overall, this application is something we are both proud to have created. The idea is salient to the both of us and is a pretty high-fidelity prototype. Our attempts to make it as interactive as possible and aesthetically pleasing surely come through. This has been one large step in the right direction for the both of us and will be a landmark in our journey as beginning designers and developers. Hopefully, we can build upon this application in the future and allow it evolve even after this assignment is submitted.
